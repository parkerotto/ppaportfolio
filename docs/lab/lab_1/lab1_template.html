2.2 Data Quality Assessment

Your Task: Calculate margin of error percentages and create reliability categories.

Requirements: - Calculate MOE percentage: (margin of error / estimate) * 100 - Create reliability categories: - High Confidence: MOE < 5% - Moderate Confidence: MOE 5-10%
- Low Confidence: MOE > 10% - Create a flag for unreliable estimates (MOE > 10%)

Hint: Use mutate() with case_when() for the categories.

# Calculate MOE percentage and reliability categories using mutate()

mo_reliability <- mo_clean %>% 
  mutate(
    moe_percentage = round((med_hh_incM / med_hh_incE) * 100, 2),
    reliability = case_when(
      moe_percentage < 5 ~ "High Confidence",
      moe_percentage >= 5 & moe_percentage <= 10 ~ "Moderate Confidence",
      moe_percentage > 10 ~ "Low Confidence"
    )
  )


# Create a summary showing count of counties in each reliability category

Part 4: Comprehensive Data Quality Evaluation

4.1 MOE Analysis for Demographic Variables

Your Task: Examine margins of error for demographic variables to see if some communities have less reliable data.

Requirements: - Calculate MOE percentages for each demographic variable - Flag tracts where any demographic variable has MOE > 15% - Create summary statistics

# Calculate MOE percentages for white, Black, and Hispanic variables
# Hint: use the same formula as before (margin/estimate * 100)

racial_reliability <- tract_clean %>% 
  mutate(
    white_moe_pct = round((white_aloneM / white_aloneE) * 100, 2),
    black_moe_pct = round((blackM / blackE) * 100, 2),
    latino_moe_pct = round((latinoM / latinoE) * 100, 2)
  )

# Create a flag for tracts with high MOE on any demographic variable
# Use logical operators (| for OR) in an ifelse() statement

racial_reliability <- racial_reliability %>% 
  mutate(
    high_moe = ifelse(
      white_moe_pct > 100 | black_moe_pct > 100 | latino_moe_pct > 100,
      "Crazy High (>100%)",
      "High (>15%)"
    )
  )

# Create summary statistics showing how many tracts have data quality issues

tract_reliability_sumstats <- racial_reliability %>% 
  group_by(high_moe) %>% 
  summarize(
    tract_count = n()
  )

5.1 Analysis Integration and Professional Summary

Your Task: Write an executive summary that integrates findings from all four analyses.

Executive Summary Requirements: 1. Overall Pattern Identification: What are the systematic patterns across all your analyses? 2. Equity Assessment: Which communities face the greatest risk of algorithmic bias based on your findings? 3. Root Cause Analysis: What underlying factors drive both data quality issues and bias risk? 4. Strategic Recommendations: What should the Department implement to address these systematic issues?

Executive Summary:

Overall, the quality of data in Rural Missouri is quite poor at both the large and small scales, as well as across median income and racial population data samples. When observing the data at the county level and using median income estimate margin of error percentages as a basis for classifying a county as having “high”, “medium”, or “low” confidence in the ACS estimation, two patterns become apparent. Spatially, confidence in the ACS estimation of median income declines the further one travels from population centers and high-infrastructure counties. St. Louis City and all of its surrounding counties within the metropolitan area have high confidence levels in their estimates at this scale, whereas Ste. Genevieve County (one county removed from the metro) has a medium confidence level and Iron county (two counties removed and bordering Ste. Genevieve) has a low confidence. Population of these counties also show a positive relationship with confidence.

To speculate about the causes of this observed decline in estimate confidence, one reason could be a lack of physical infrastructure. While I-55 bisects both Ste. Genevieve and Jefferson Counties, there is no interstate present in Iron County (the entire area is serviced only by 1-lane-per-direction state roads). Additionally, many people in all three counties (although far more in Iron and Ste. Gen than Jefferson) live off of dirt roads and lack access to reliable (if any at all) internet and cellular coverage. Growing up in this area, this was considered normal (many of my peers would get around by ATV / four-wheeler when roads simply did not exist) and it was not until I moved to Chicago for undergrad that I learned of comparisons of my home region of the Ozarks to Appalachia. The presence of infrastructure too divides the Ozarks (to the point where those along the I-55 corridor don’t even necessarily call themselves Ozarkians, also known as Hillbillies and this is the region where the term originated—although we call them “Hoosiers” in our local dialect, much to the confusion of my friends in undergrad from Indiana). Needless to say, the lack of digital and physical infrastructure may also have an impact on the area’s overall educational attainment. Since the ACS relies on a small sample of people to answer their survey (which is a very small number of people spread across a large geography), and the area lacks funded educational systems (in part due to its Republican leadership at every level of government, which is constantly threatening to cut funding entirely to libraries and schools that refuse state-sponsored censorship of People of Color and Queer voices) the people receiving ACS surveys may not know how to effectively or accurately fill them out.

At the Census Tract level, even aforementioned high-confidence counties (determined through median income MOE) can show great variation in confidence levels among racial groups. Latino and Black population estimates are always greater than 50% MOE percent across tracts in Jefferson, Ste. Genevieve, and Iron counties (listed based on distance from St. Louis County, which is the collar county to St. Louis City). This high MOE percent for people of color is likely due to the extremely low populations of both of these groups. Black population estimates at the tract level range from 0 to 377 while Latino estimates range from 0 to 498. The estimates of 0 in these cases also cause a large chunk of the data to be unusable for calculating averages in Part 4.2, as the percentage calculation results in infinite values.

In terms of recommendations, I am unfortunately pessimistic that the ACS or broader Census could do anything to improve the situation. The issues of sample size in racial data are due to the fact that, in many of these extremely rural tracts, there just aren’t any people of color at all. Having grown up biracial in this environment, I know first-hand that it is an incredibly hostile culture and environment to live in for people of color and many go out of their way to avoid traveling to (let alone settling down in) these counties. Education is tough too given that school funding is largely tied to local property values. While there are small pockets of good public schools in the Ozarks, places like Iron County that have both widespread poverty and a low population will likely never see substantial investment in K-12 education under this system (or even the widely proposed alternative of money-per-student). Infrastructure, however, is the one area that could be the most feasible to improve both economically and socially. Having fast internet is, after all, a fairly non-partisan issue. Roads, too, are quite popular across the isle, but the ever-increasing bill for suburban expansion in the St. Louis and Kansas City metros eats up most of the state budget. One of these counties, however, (Jefferson) is seeing billions in state and federal infrastructure investment via a highway widening project (2 to 3 lanes one-way) on I-55 that will improve mobility for the region and, therefore, access to opportunity and resources. Iron County, however, will see none of this. Partnering with a private company (or even creating a public broadband option, however unlikely that may be in such a ruby red state) could greatly boost the literacy (and therefore potentially the quality of ACS responses) of Iron County. This would, however, require investment based on humanity and not based on growth or profit, as this area is unlikely to see any migration from the ever-expanding St. Louis Metro (the reason why Jefferson County is getting so much public money right now) unless a brand new interstate were to be built through it (which will, yet again, likely never happen). One possibility that is completely within the reach of the U.S. Census is to proctor in-person surveys (at a polling place, church, etc). This way professionals can be available to provide clarity for questions that may be confusing. This can be paired with expanded sample sizes to further increase confidence in the estimates.

Technical Notes

Data Sources: - U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 2018-2022 5-Year Estimates - Retrieved via tidycensus R package on 2-9-2026

Reproducibility: - All analysis conducted in R version 4.3.1 (2023-06-16) - Census API key required for replication - Complete code and documentation available at: https://parkerotto.github.io/ppaportfolio/lab/lab_1/lab1_template.html

Methodology Notes: Given the low number of Black and Latino people in certain tracts, some MOE percentages were populated with infinite values. I chose to drop these to create an average for the over 100% MOE tracts that was comparable to the under 100% MOE tracts.

Limitations: The biggest limitation with this workflow was the chosen geography. The racial variables for these counties and tracts do not produce effective MOE calculations due to their extraordinarily low numbers of people of color living there.
